▶ Tax deduction limitation

The IRS implements tax deductions by reducing your taxable income. In all cases, this means your tax deductions are limited by the total taxes you pay, but for donations, the IRS has stricter limits than that. The goal of this section is to give a realistic picture of what your total tax deduction might be, and a sense of the tax planning needed to get that deduction.

Here is a graph of the maximum tax deduction you can expect, using the share numbers above. The x-axis is the total (household) income you expect over the next 10 years, not including income you get from exercising or selling company shares. Include both wage income and investment income.

Some caveats: this graph is less battle-tested than the table above, and so could straight up have mistakes. There are other strategies not represented that could increase the line (e.g. [8]). And finally, AMT considerations could change the ideal strategy.

Caveats aside, below is the minimum amount of planning you may want to do, if it'll take Y years of income to get to $ (minimum income needed to realize max deduction):

  • If your total pre-match donations are less than 1-2x your household income: Nothing special is needed.
  • If Y < 5: Any time you sell, donate at least your pledge %age in that calendar year.
  • If 5 < Y < 10: The above, plus space out donations (see below).
  • If Y > 10: The above, plus make cost basis elections (see below).

The planning involved when Y > 5 can get complex, and it's natural to wonder how much you'd "lose" if you only followed the relatively simple Y < 5 advice. I would say: it's not material enough to be relevant for a pledge decision, but material enough that I'd recommend hiring someone (should cost < $10k/yr) to deal with it in the case that your shares are worth enough for this to be a problem.

The rest of this section is going to get into the weeds a bit, since it's necessary context if you're going to DIY it. If you're not, feel free to skip.

Doing the computation

DAFs are "50% limit organizations", in the parlance of the IRS. 50% limit organizations have the following deduction limits:

Holding period Annual deduction limit, as a % of income
Cashless exercise during liquidity 50% [5]
Dispose <1 yr after exercise 50%
Dispose 1+ yr after exercise 30% if using sale price, or 50% with cost basis election (see below)

Extra donations carry forward for 5 years. So if you make $100k/yr, and donate in cash, you can donate $300k in year 1 (50% * 100k * 6 years), and have it all (eventually) be deducted.

Cost basis election for NSOs and ISOs

This is only relevant if you're not likely to hit the Row 4 value in the table above. Almost certainly, that means you have some Long D, and very likely it means you will end up fully saturating your 30% deductions, but not saturating your 50% deductions.

In that scenario, you can elect to have your Long Ds fall under the 50% limit, in exchange for the deductable amount being smaller. Here are the deductible amounts under each limit:

Under 30% limit Under 50% limit
Long NSO Sale price Exercise price
Long ISO Sale price Strike price

As you can see, NSOs that get switched over are worth more than ISOs, which is why the graph above suggests switching over NSOs before switching over ISOs.

An important note is that if you elect to switch your 30% donations to the 50% limit, you must do it for all your 30% donations that year. The election also applies to all carryover from that year, though it doesn't apply to donations initiated in future years. [6] You cannot amend a tax return to retroactively change this election, in either direction.

If you expect to end up in this situation, the planning implications are a bit complicated. It may imply that you donate only NSOs or only ISOs in a particular year, or that you don't bother early exercising shares that you're exercising only to donate. Some simplifying observations: it's still basically always better to exercise ISOs (vs NSOs) if you have the choice. And Short donations are still (far) worse than Long donations, even with the election, since even though the deduction is the same, with Short donations you owe taxes on the gain.

Spacing donations out

The 5 < Y < 10 case in the intro mentions spacing out your donations. Donations carry forward for only 5 years, after which anything that hasn't been deducted is lost. So with a sufficiently large donation, it can be beneficial to split it over multiple years, so that it can be deducted over more than 5 years of income. Some additional thoughts:

  • You have three years after a sale to make the donation (company rule, not tax law), which should help with this.
  • You always have to use the current year's donations before you can dip into your carryover, even if the carryover is about to expire. In the extreme case, let's say you make a 10x-your-income donation in year 1. You would need to delay any further donations till year 7 to get a deduction benefit for them.
  • You may want to front-load your donations (e.g. start donating this year), to start the clock / take advantage of this year's income.

Finally, an unrelated reminder not to let the tax tail wag the impact dog. The benefit of the tax deduction is material, but may or may not be enough to outweigh being able to donate earlier, if you have a concrete reason to do so.

Some reminders

  • If you switch tax accountants or tax software, make sure to copy over your charitable contribution carryovers. Most accountants will not think to ask for it.
  • Do not donate shares that have lost value (i.e. where sale price < exercise price). Sell the shares and donate the cash. See "Cashflow and risk considerations" for further discussion.

▶ Cashflow and risk considerations

From the table above, you can see that exercising in advance of a sale/donation (especially 1+ year in advance) is significantly more favorable than cashless exercise. So why are we even talking about cashless exercise?

The two main considerations with exercising in advance are that you have to put up money before you get anything in return (cashflow), and that you risk the loss of the money you put up (what I'm calling risk). Details of the calculation are below.

Cashflow

  • When you exercise NSOs, you owe "strike price + income tax * spread" ($15/share) at the time of exercise. Some of this you pay in Carta at the time of exercise, and the rest you pay at tax time (appears on your W-2).
  • When you exercise ISOs, you owe "strike price" ($10/share) for sure, and then possibly also owe AMT on the spread. You do not pay AMT at the time of exercise in Carta; it only appears at tax time (Form 3921).

The relevance to cashflow is that even if things go well, you won't have access to this money until there is a liquidity event and you sell.

Risk

If sale price >= exercise price (i.e. if gain >= 0), then exercising early is always at least as good on net as cashless exercise (so, nothing to worry about). However, if gain < 0, then exercising early will end up coming out worse than cashless exercise.

How sales work:

  • In the extreme case, if the sale price is 0, you lose everything you paid at time of exercise, and get a capital loss of "exercise price" that you can carry forward indefinitely, to offset future capital gains.
  • In general, when gain < 0, you did worse than if you had cashless exercised, but are only out money on net if sale price < "what you paid when you exercised" ($15/share or $10/share + AMT for NSO/ISO respectively). At the time of sale you'll get a free capital loss of "-gain" that you can carry forward indefinitely.
  • One exception is that if you sell an ISO in the same year that you exercise, the AMT is "forgiven", so it'll just be as if you cashless exercised at the time you sold.

For donations:

  • If gain < 0, you should sell and donate the money in almost all cases, instead of directly donating the shares. If you do end up donating directly, in the very best case, it's similar to selling and donating the cash, except you don't get the free capital loss. The worst case is if you donate ISOs that have lost value <1 yr after exercise; they turn into NSOs and I believe you now retroactively owe income tax on the spread.

[1] I decided to elide differences of a few percent and rare-ish technicalities in favor of simplification. I'm happy to go over any particular (US/California-centric) tax situation if you think there may be a technicality that might apply!

[2] The match will likely be in shares, but easier to reason about as the cash-equivalent.

[3] The actual deduction could be significantly less, especially if the company does well, and you donate ISOs or NSOs you hold for longer than a year. See the tax deduction limitation section for a handy graph. A few additional caveats:

  • If you would normally take the standard deduction, subtract $2-3k (single) or $7-9k (married) per year that you donate, since you give up on the standard deduction when you take this one.
  • If you live in California and have high income, subtract $700 per $100k you make above $250k (single) or $500k (married) per year that you donate (see "Reduction in itemized deductions" here).
  • SALT is deductible under regular income, but not under AMT, and the marginal AMT tax due to the SALT adjustment does not result in credits (see the treatment of exclusion items vs deferral items in Form 8801). So if exercising ISOs pushes you into paying AMT at all, you'll likely end up with an unrecoverable tax bill of $3-4k each year you pay AMT.

[4] I am not sure if you also owe AMT on the ISOs, in some cases. If you do, the amount of AMT will be "amt * spread"

[5] This deserves its own calculator, but at a high level, each year you work and have a "normal" tax situation you generate tens of thousands of "AMT credits". Ignoring questions of cash flow, you only end up paying AMT if your (lifetime, post-year-of-exercise) AMT credits don't cover the AMT you owe. For now, I've excluded AMT from Totals, and haven't subtracted it from "what you get in cash", even though you are likely to owe lifetime AMT in worlds where the company does well and you donate a large fraction of your shares. Also, AMT rates range from 26% to 42%, depending on your income and state; I arbitrarily used 35% here.

[6] It's actually 60% federally for 2025 (still 50% for California).

[7] The election also applies to carryovers from past years in some way, though I don't know the details.

[8] It seems possible (but I haven’t verified) that one can elect to realize income on the full sale value of short term option donations, and also deduct that value under the 50% limit. This would help in the common case where you have too many 30% donations and not enough 50% donations, since increasing your income increases the number of 30% donations you can make.
